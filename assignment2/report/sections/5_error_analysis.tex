To better understand the remaining errors, we examined the first 20 misclassified test examples saved for each of the six runs. Rather than looking at isolated mistakes, we focused on examples that reappeared across multiple models and sequence lengths. This makes it possible to identify systematic weaknesses instead of noise from a single run. Full listings of all misclassified examples are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:misclass}.

The clearest pattern is confusion between \textit{Sci/Tech} and \textit{Business}. Across the saved error files, \textit{Sci/Tech} articles misclassified as \textit{Business} is the single most common error type, followed by the reverse. This is unsurprising: many technology articles in AG News employ business language such as \textit{market}, \textit{shares}, \textit{growth}, or \textit{workers}, and frequently reference company names. Sci/Tech articles that were misclassified as Business include ``IBM to hire even more new workers'' (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 5), ``Dell Exits Low-End China Consumer PC Market'' (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 8), and a Google IPO article (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 6). Conversely, Business articles mislabeled as Sci/Tech include ``Intel to delay product aimed for high-definition TVs'' (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 11), ``Yahoo! Ups Ante for Small Businesses'' (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 15), and ``Surviving Biotech's Downturns'' (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 13). These systematic errors reveal that the boundary between technological innovation and economic reporting is inherently ambiguous, especially in news data.

Another recurring pattern is confusion between \textit{World} and \textit{Business}. Articles about politics or international affairs are often misclassified as \textit{Business} when they mention oil prices, trade, or financial consequences. Examples include ``Oil prices bubble to record high'' (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 16), ``Venezuela Prepares for Chavez Recall Vote'' (App.~\ref{app:run2}, Example 7), and ``Google Lowers Its IPO Price Range'' (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 18). In these cases, the model seems to rely strongly on economic keywords, even when the broader topic is political or international. This suggests that topical overlap between global affairs and economics remains difficult for both architectures.

We also observe a smaller but consistent confusion between \textit{World} and \textit{Sports}. For instance, ``Live: Olympics day four'' (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 10) is repeatedly predicted as \textit{Sports} although it is labelled as \textit{World}, and political or public-affairs articles such as ``Mr Downer shoots his mouth off'' (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 14) are sometimes predicted as \textit{Sports}. These mistakes suggest that short headlines without much context can be difficult to classify, especially when they contain named entities or event-style phrasing that does not clearly reveal the topic.

Some errors are caused by lexical ambiguity inside very short texts. The article ``E-mail scam targets police chief'' (App.~\ref{app:run2}, Example 2) was misclassified as \textit{World} in multiple runs, probably because words such as \textit{police} and \textit{chief} are also common in political or crime-related world news. Similarly, ``Super ant colony hits Australia'' (App.~\ref{app:run1}, Example 3) was often predicted as \textit{World}, even though it belongs to \textit{Sci/Tech}. In these examples, the model appears to focus on surface cues such as country names or institutional words rather than the underlying scientific content.

Comparing the two models, the overall error types are very similar, but the CNN tends to make slightly fewer mistakes on the hardest category boundaries. The LSTM shares most of the same repeated errors, especially the \textit{Sci/Tech}--\textit{Business} confusions, which suggests that additional sequence modelling alone is not enough to solve these cases. Many of the problematic examples are difficult because they are genuinely mixed-topic articles rather than simple classification failures.

The saved errors also show that several examples are misclassified in almost every run. Articles about IBM, Yahoo!, Intel, oil prices, and Google IPOs appear repeatedly across both models and all sequence lengths, indicating that these cases reflect persistent ambiguities in the dataset rather than random noise from individual runs.

Overall, the error analysis suggests that the main challenge in this task is separating articles that sit near the boundary between two related topics. In particular, the distinction between \textit{Sci/Tech} and \textit{Business} remains the most difficult for both models. This is consistent with the class-wise results reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}, where Business and Sci/Tech were the lowest-performing categories.
