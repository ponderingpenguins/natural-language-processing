Our results suggest that this task depends a lot on short local patterns in the text. That helps explain why the CNN is already strong at sequence length 64: even with shorter inputs, it can pick up useful topic phrases and keywords. So the CNN advantage at short length is not only a score difference, but also a good match between the model and the kind of signal in AG News.

At the same time, the BiLSTM improves more as we increase sequence length, and at 256 tokens it slightly surpasses the CNN (Table~\ref{tab:test_results}; 90.74\% vs 90.72\% macro-F1). A likely reason is that the extra context helps the LSTM in examples where early words are not enough to decide the class. This seems especially relevant for Business and Sci/Tech, where articles often contain mixed vocabulary. Even so, the best overall configuration is still CNN-seq64, which suggests that local cues remain very important for this dataset.

The class-wise results support this broader picture. World and Sports are generally easier for both models, while Business and Sci/Tech remain harder. This tells us that the remaining errors are mostly about separating semantically close topics, not about basic model failure.

Overall, the results do not show that simply increasing sequence length leads to better performance. For the CNN, the best test result was reached at seq64, while for the LSTM the best result was reached at seq256. This difference suggests that the two architectures use context differently: the CNN appears to benefit most from short, highly informative local patterns, whereas the LSTM gains somewhat more from additional context. However, the remaining errors in both models are very similar, which indicates that the main limitation is not sequence length alone, but the difficulty of separating overlapping news categories such as Business and Sci/Tech.
