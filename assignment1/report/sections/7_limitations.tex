Our results point to two interconnected limitations. The first is that TF-IDF only counts how often words appear, with no understanding of how they are used. This directly explains the overlapping keywords errors we identified: if an article about a tech company also contains a lot of financial vocabulary, the model may classify it as business simply because those terms score higher, even if the article is primarily about technology. The same issue appears in the "simply difficult" category, where the overall topic of the article was not reflected in its most frequent words. A fine-tuned transformer would address both problems by encoding meaning in context; static word embeddings like Word2Vec would help with synonymy but would not solve the context problem.

The second limitation is the linearity of our classifiers. The t-SNE projections show substantial overlap between the business and sci/tech clusters, which a linear decision boundary cannot cleanly resolve. Non-linear models could in principle learn more expressive boundaries in this region. However, our ablation results suggest that the representation is the primary bottleneck: the gains available from preprocessing changes were modest, which implies that the ceiling imposed by the bag-of-words assumption matters more than the choice of classifier. Switching to a transformer encoder would address both limitations simultaneously, at the cost of the interpretable feature analysis shown in Figure \ref{fig:linear_svm_top_features}.
