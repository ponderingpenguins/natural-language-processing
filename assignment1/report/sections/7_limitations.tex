One limitation of our approach is that we used TF-IDF features, which are based on word frequency and do not capture the semantic meaning of the text. This can lead to issues with polysemy (where a word has multiple meanings) and synonymy (where different words have similar meanings), which can cause misclassifications. Future work could explore richer feature representations such as word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) or contextual encodings (e.g., BERT, sentence transformers) that can better capture semantic similarity and polysemy. These representations could potentially improve the performance of the models by providing more informative features that capture the meaning of the text rather than just the frequency of words.

Another limitation is that we used linear models (Logistic Regression and Linear SVM), which may not be able to capture complex relationships in the data. The t-SNE projections of the test set predictions showed that the classes are not perfectly separable, suggesting that there may be non-linear decision boundaries that could better separate the classes. Future work could explore non-linear models such as kernel SVM, random forests, or neural classifiers that could potentially exploit more complex decision boundaries. However, it is important to note that these models may come with trade-offs in terms of interpretability, computational cost, and risk of overfitting, especially on a dataset of this size. Careful tuning and evaluation would be necessary to confirm any gains over the linear baseline.
